# CFO Playbook — Decision Frameworks

Distilled decision frameworks organized by domain. Quick reference for applying the right mental model.

---

## MFCF Framework

**The line**: Everything above = cash generation from existing operations. Everything below = capital allocation.

```
EBITDA
- SBC (as cash cost)
- Maintenance CapEx
- Maintenance R&D
± Working Capital movements
- Cash Interest
- Cash Tax
= MFCF

--- THE LINE ---

- Growth CapEx
- Growth R&D
- Acquisitions
- Dividends / Buybacks
= Capital Allocation Decisions
```

**North star**: Maximize MFCF per share over time.

---

## The Coffee Machine Test

**Question**: "If we stopped this spend, what happens to current revenue?"
- Revenue falls → Maintenance CapEx
- Revenue grows → Growth CapEx
- Unclear → Dig harder

**Application**: Any recurring spend on existing assets passes the test if stopping it causes operational deterioration.

---

## Capital Allocation Decision Hierarchy

1. **Fix the balance sheet** (if impaired — broken leg principle: can't run before you can walk)
2. **Maintain operations** (maintenance capex + WC fully funded)
3. **Fund growth** (returns exceed cost of capital — capex, opex, M&A evaluated on same basis)
4. **Return capital** (if no better use — buybacks, dividends, debt repayment)

**Core principle**: Return capital unless you can generate better returns per dollar than shareholders can elsewhere.

## CapEx Envelope Decision Process

```
Step 1: Size the envelope
  ↓ Access to capital? Cost of capital? Quality of pipeline? Execution ability?
Step 2: Classify projects
  ↓ Hard return (proven) vs Soft return (speculative)
Step 3: Evaluate
  ↓ Payback period (primary) + NPV per $ invested (secondary)
Step 4: Build probability distributions
  ↓ Hard return: tight distribution → single-point estimate reliable
  ↓ Soft return: wide distribution → scenario range with probabilities
Step 5: Stage-gate soft returns
  ↓ Gate 1: exploratory → Gate 2: pilot → Gate 3: full commitment
Step 6: Burn the Boats
  ↓ Formal approval with full accountability
Step 7: Track and PIR
  ↓ On Time + On Budget → Did it deliver the EBITDA?
```

## Poker-Not-Chess

Approving capex is not chess (all cards visible). It's poker (hidden cards in play).

**Implication**: Don't evaluate at point estimates. Build probability-weighted outcome distributions.

**Hard returns** → tight distribution → confident in base case
**Soft returns** → wide distribution → scenario range, probability weights, explicit downside

## Working Capital Flywheel

**Business A** (positive NWC): Growth consumes cash → needs funding → constrained growth
**Business B** (negative NWC): Growth generates cash → self-funding → compounding flywheel

```
CCC = DIO + DSO - DPO

Negative CCC → Business B
Positive CCC → Business A

Business B example:
  DPO 75 days, DIO 15 days, DSO 0 days
  CCC = -60 days
  New $10m store → $5m cash inflow in 90 days
```

**Why suppliers fund it**: They have stronger balance sheets and cheaper funding; they're happy to fund buyer's growth.

## QoE Adjustment Line Test

| Lines | Signal |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Clean earnings |
| 4–7 | Normal — scrutinize each |
| 8–12 | Concern — management constructing a number |
| 12+ | Red flag — EBITDA is a management construct |

**For each line**: Is it genuinely non-recurring? Would an independent auditor accept it? Does it appear in multiple periods?

## The 4–6 Things Framework

Before any DD engagement: list the deal assumptions that drive your valuation. If any are wrong by a mile, the thesis breaks. These become the DD priority list.

**Apply to each assumption**:
- What DD work stream tests this assumption?
- What's the cost of getting it wrong?
- What confidence level do we need before signing?

Cut every advisor work stream that doesn't test one of the 4–6 things.

## WC Peg Strategy

**Buyer**: Set peg HIGH (want more WC delivered at close)
**Seller**: Set peg LOW (want to deliver less WC)

**Strategy**: Defer negotiation until late in the process. Build detailed monthly NWC schedule. Challenge what "normal" means in the 12-month average.

**Value**: Worth 1–3% of enterprise value.

## Capital Desperation Trap

```
Business underperforms plan
    ↓
CFADS insufficient to refinance at market rates
    ↓
Unable to attract reasonable lenders
    ↓
Forced into distressed/vulture funding at punishing rates
    ↓
Capital desperation compounds underperformance
```

**Avoid by**: Sizing leverage conservatively. Maintaining covenant headroom of 20%+. Having RCF as liquidity buffer. Building PIK toggle into structure.

## CFADS / Debt Capacity

```
CFADS = EBITDA - Cash Tax - Maintenance CapEx - WC movements - Mandatory amortization

Debt Capacity = CFADS ÷ Target DSCR

Conservative DSCR: 1.5x
Minimum acceptable: 1.2x
Danger zone: <1.1x
```

**Cycle adjustment**: For cyclical businesses, size debt to CFADS at trough EBITDA, not peak.

## Deal Failure Categories

**Wrong Target** → Commercial/strategic fit failures
**Wrong Deal** → Overpaying, insufficient DD, poor terms, bad timing
**Wrong Execution** → Integration failure, undelivered synergies, wrong team

~70–90% of acquisitions destroy value. Most failures are preventable with discipline on the 4–6 things and honest execution assessment.

## Behavioral Bias Triggers (and Tests)

| Bias | Trigger | Test |
|---|---|---|
| Anchoring | Management plan is the base case | "What would we project from history alone?" |
| Sunk cost | $500k DD spent, deal losing merit | "Would we start DD today with what we know?" |
| Confirmation | DD validating, not challenging thesis | "What would make us walk away?" |
| Winner's curse | Won competitive process | "Why did we win? What did others see that we missed?" |
| Optimism bias | Synergies at 100% delivery in Year 1 | "What's our historical synergy delivery hit rate?" |

## The "Would a Skeptical CFO Be Comfortable?" Test

Final check before IC recommendation:
- Is LTM EBITDA real, recurring, and representative?
- Has capex been maintained, or is there a cliff we're buying into?
- What happens if the top 3 customers leave?
- Can we articulate why we won and why we're right?
- Is the management team capable of executing at PE pace?
- Is the integration plan fully resourced?
- Is there a credible bear case where this is still a good deal?
- Are we proceeding because the deal is good, or because we've spent too much to stop?
