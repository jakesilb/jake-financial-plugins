# Common Pitfalls — Cross-Cutting CFO Red Flag Detection

This skill applies the final layer of pattern recognition across all domains. After the individual skills have done their work, this overlay asks: what are we missing? What are the behavioral traps we're falling into? Where are the systemic failure patterns that destroy PE value?

**Proactive use**: Run this as the final check before an IC recommendation. Ask: "Would a skeptical, experienced CFO be comfortable with this deal?"

## Step 2: The Three Categories of Deal Failure

70–90% of acquisitions destroy value (HBS research). Failures cluster into three categories:

### Category 1: Wrong Target
- Bad strategic fit with portfolio or thesis
- Market is smaller/slower than assumed
- Competitive dynamics more intense than understood
- Business is not what it appears (earnings quality, key person dependency)

### Category 2: Wrong Deal
- Overpaying relative to fundamentals (EBITDA quality not stress-tested)
- Insufficient diligence on key assumptions
- Wrong timing (buying at cycle peak; sector in structural decline)
- Poor contractual protections (weak reps and warranties, no seller note, no WC peg)
- Bad advice from advisors who benefit from deal completion

### Category 3: Wrong Execution
- Integration failure (Playing Lego without an integration playbook)
- Unrealized synergies (promised but never tracked or owned)
- Value creation plan too ambitious or not resourced
- Management team wrong for PE pace/intensity
- Core business neglected while chasing growth

## Step 3: Behavioral Bias Checklist

These biases operate silently. Identify which ones are present in the current deal.

### Anchoring to Management Projections
- **What happens**: Management presents a 3-year plan at deal entry. Everyone anchors to it. DD is designed to validate it rather than challenge it.
- **Test**: "If management said nothing, what would we project based on the historical data alone?" Compare to management's case.
- **Red flag**: Management case is 30%+ above the extrapolated historical trend with no clear inflection point explanation.

### Sunk Cost Fallacy in DD Spend
- **What happens**: $500k into DD, deal is losing momentum, but the team keeps going because of what's already been spent.
- **Test**: "If we were starting DD today with full knowledge of what we've found so far, would we still do it?"
- **Red flag**: Decision to continue is primarily driven by not wanting to "waste" the DD spend.

### Confirmation Bias
- **What happens**: Thesis is formed early. DD is conducted to confirm it, not challenge it. Contrary findings are explained away.
- **Test**: "What would it take for us to walk away?" If the answer is "nothing short of fraud," confirmation bias is active.
- **Red flag**: Every concern raised in DD gets a management response that gets accepted without independent verification.

### Winner's Curse (Auction Dynamics)
- **What happens**: In competitive processes, the winner overpays by definition — they outbid everyone who had the same information.
- **Test**: "Why did we win? What did we see that others missed?" If the answer is unclear, assume the market priced it correctly.
- **Red flag**: Won a competitive auction without a clear proprietary advantage in thesis or information.

### Optimism Bias in Synergies
- **What happens**: Synergies are assumed, not proven. They get baked into the model as certainty.
- **Test**: "Have we tracked the delivery of synergies from our last 3 acquisitions? What was the hit rate?"
- **Red flag**: No historical synergy delivery data; synergies are in the model from Day 1 with no stage-gate.

## Step 4: Cross-Domain Red Flag Patterns

### The Nathan Problem (Capital Destruction)
- Significant growth capex or acquisition spend deployed over multiple years
- EBITDA is flat or declining over the same period
- Management explanation: "investments take time to mature"
- CFO test: Calculate growth capex deployed ÷ EBITDA change. If implied CROI is below cost of capital and no clear inflection in sight → capital destruction, not value creation
- **When to walk**: If the Nathan pattern has been running for 3+ years without a credible catalyst, the return will not materialize

### Strategy–Capital Disconnect
- Management articulates a growth strategy
- Capital allocation shows maintenance-heavy spend, no growth investment
- Or: management claims "capital discipline" but approves every project that clears a 5% IRR
- **Test**: Map where cash has actually gone over the last 3 years vs the stated strategy. If they don't align, one of them is wrong.

### The "Every Project Is NPV Positive" Problem
- Every business case submitted for approval shows a positive NPV
- Hurdle rates exist on paper but are not enforced in practice
- Models are built backward from the desired answer
- **Test**: Ask for the list of rejected projects over the last 2 years. If there are none, the hurdle rate isn't real.

### WACC Manipulation
- Low discount rate buries a bad investment in a positive NPV
- WACC of 8% applied to a PE-backed, cyclical, mid-market business is absurd (real cost of equity is 15–20%+)
- "This project has an IRR of 14% using a 9% WACC" — strip out the WACC fiction and the real return is often sub-cost-of-capital
- **Test**: Apply a skeptical hurdle rate (15–20% for growth capex; 12%+ for acquisitions). Does the project still work?

### Playing Lego Without Integration Capability
- PE firm with an aggressive buy-and-build thesis
- No integration playbook, no dedicated integration resource, no synergy tracking
- Acquisitions close faster than integration can keep up
- **Pattern**: Revenue grows (deals close), but margins compress (integration cost + complexity + management distraction)
- **Test**: Map the last 3 acquisitions: cost vs budget, timeline vs plan, synergies delivered vs projected

### Lemon Squeezed Too Hard
- Squeezing the Lemon pillar is working — margins improving
- But: SG&A cut below sustainable run-rate, R&D gutted, headcount too thin
- Short-term EBITDA inflates → exit story looks great → buyer inherits a business that can't maintain the margin
- **Test**: Compare SG&A as % of revenue to the 5-year historical range and industry benchmark. If it's at the bottom of the range after cost cuts, the margin is fragile.

### Polish the Trophy Without the Substance
- Exit story is compelling — narrative, reporting, management team, market positioning
- But: underlying EBITDA isn't real (high adjustment count) or sustainable (margin propped by cost cuts)
- Buyer DD will unwind the story
- **Test**: Strip the EBITDA normalization to only adjustments a skeptical buyer would accept. What's the "clean" number?

## Step 5: Industry-Specific Pitfall Overlay

### SaaS / Software
- Mistaking revenue growth for unit economics health (ARR up, NRR down, CAC increasing)
- Professional services revenue mixed into SaaS metrics inflating the multiple
- Capitalized dev costs overstating margins and understating cashflow consumption
- SBC addback creating phantom earnings quality
- Cohort economics degrading (logo retention fine, expansion masking churn)

### Manufacturing / Industrial
- Deferred maintenance capex cliff — buying at the end of an asset replacement cycle
- Environmental liability not surfaced in standard DD
- Cyclical peak EBITDA used as the entry basis — trough will look nothing like the model
- Single-source supplier dependency not identified until post-close

### Business Services
- Key person dependency vastly underestimated (one person IS the business)
- Client concentration: top 3 clients at 70%+ = binary revenue risk
- Non-competes unenforceable in the relevant jurisdiction
- Utilization rate at unsustainable peak (seller has been over-billing hours pre-sale)

### Retail / Consumer
- Same-store sales declining while new stores mask it in aggregate revenue
- Inventory aging / markdown risk not reflected in stated margins
- Franchise vs company-owned unit mix changing in ways that distort comparables
- Channel shift to e-commerce destroying the economics of existing store base

### Healthcare
- Reimbursement rate changes can shift the entire cashflow profile overnight
- Regulatory compliance deficiencies emerge post-close (not disclosed in DD)
- Payer mix shifting toward lower-reimbursement sources
- Staffing cost inflation (especially nursing/clinical) not in the model

## Step 6: The "Would a Skeptical CFO Be Comfortable?" Test

Run through these final questions before recommending:

**On the business:**
- [ ] Is the LTM EBITDA real, recurring, and representative of the go-forward business?
- [ ] Has capex been maintained, or is there a cliff we're buying into?
- [ ] Is the WC cycle a structural strength or a timing illusion?
- [ ] What happens to the business if the top 3 customers leave?

**On the deal:**
- [ ] Can we articulate specifically why we won (if competitive process) and why we're right?
- [ ] Have we stress-tested the key assumptions — not just sensitized the model?
- [ ] Are the contractual protections (reps, WC peg, seller note) adequate for the risks found in DD?
- [ ] Is there a credible bear case where this is still a good deal?

**On execution:**
- [ ] Is the management team capable of executing the value creation plan at PE pace?
- [ ] Is the integration plan fully resourced, not just sketched?
- [ ] Are synergies owned by a specific person, or are they floating in the model?
- [ ] What's our exit thesis, and does it depend on market conditions we can't control?

**On ourselves:**
- [ ] Are we anchored to management's numbers, or have we built our own independent view?
- [ ] Would we continue this DD if we were starting today?
- [ ] Are we proceeding because the deal is genuinely good, or because we've spent so much time on it?

## Step 7: Structured Output

### What We Like
- e.g., "Deal team independently stress-tested all 5 key assumptions vs management case — confident in the earnings quality"
- e.g., "Track record on integration: last 3 add-ons integrated within 90 days, synergies delivered within 10% of projection"

### What We Don't Like
- e.g., "Management projects 35% EBITDA growth over 3 years vs historical 8% CAGR — no clear inflection driver identified; classic optimism bias"
- e.g., "Won the auction; unclear why we won over two other well-resourced PE firms with the same information; winner's curse risk"

### Where Is the Opportunity
- e.g., "The bear case (20% EBITDA decline, multiple compression) still generates 1.5x — there's real downside protection here"
- e.g., "Management team has never worked at PE pace — upgrading CFO is a value creation lever in itself; worth building into the plan"

### Red Flags — Final Checklist

Cross-cutting flags requiring resolution before IC:
- [ ] Nathan problem: capex/acquisition spend without matching EBITDA improvement
- [ ] Strategy–capital disconnect confirmed
- [ ] No history of rejected projects (hurdle rates not real)
- [ ] WACC being used to justify inadequate returns
- [ ] Key person risk not contractually mitigated
- [ ] Integration capability assessed as inadequate for the buying pace
- [ ] LTM EBITDA heavily adjusted (>8 lines)
- [ ] Behavioral bias (anchoring, sunk cost, confirmation) identified and not corrected for
- [ ] Exit thesis depends entirely on market conditions outside our control
